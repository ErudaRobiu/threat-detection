# SUMMARY, CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

## 5.1 Summary of the Study

This study set out to design and implement an AI-driven cybersecurity threat detection system that fuses rule-based structural analysis with large-language-model semantic analysis under a deny-by-default security posture, and to evaluate that system against standalone rule-based and standalone semantic detection. The system was built as a single Next.js application whose server-side routes host an analysis core of five modules: a preprocessor that redacts links and addresses so that the semantic layer reads only language, a rule engine that scores nine weighted structural indicators, a semantic module that issues a single constrained call to the Gemini model, a fusion module that combines the two scores through the Hybrid Threat Scoring Algorithm, and a report generator. The core was covered by 67 passing unit tests, persisted its results to a local database, and degraded gracefully to rule-only scoring when the semantic service was unavailable.

The system was then evaluated by a Python harness against benchmark corpora of phishing and legitimate URLs and emails. The run was truncated for time before the full corpus completed, so the detection results rest on 857 cleanly scored items. Across these, the rule layer achieved high precision and low recall, the fusion produced a small improvement in precision and false-positive rate over its unfused counterpart, and the machine-learning baseline, once corrected for a collection artefact, achieved a modest F1 of 0.727. The study also identified and corrected five measurement artefacts, derived an escalation refinement to the fusion from first principles, and established by formal analysis that the interaction term at the centre of the algorithm behaves in the opposite manner to the one originally proposed.

## 5.2 Conclusion

The study concludes that a hybrid of structural and semantic analysis under a deny-by-default posture is a sound architecture for content-level threat detection, not because it produced the highest benchmark figure, which on this truncated corpus it did not, but because it makes each verdict inspectable, decomposes every score into its structural and semantic contributions, and fails toward caution rather than toward clearance. The two layers were shown to fail in mirror-image ways and to rescue one another's blind spots, which is the substantive case for fusing them.

The character of the interaction term was clarified during implementation, and this conclusion records that clarification deliberately. The term was conceived, and described in the original design, as an amplifier that raises the combined score when the two analytical layers agree. Formal analysis established the opposite. Because the fusion weights sum to unity and the product of two scores in the unit interval never exceeds their mean, the hybrid score can never exceed the mean of its components; the interaction term does not amplify agreement but withholds part of the score range and returns it in proportion to agreement. The term is an agreement gate, a conjunctive fusion whose purpose is the suppression of false positives. Owning this correction in the conclusion, rather than presenting the mechanism as though it had behaved as first assumed, is a deliberate choice, because the analysis that produced the correction, and the mixed evaluation of the gate that followed it, are themselves among the contributions of the work.

## 5.3 Contributions to Knowledge

This study makes five contributions. First, it presents a formal fusion of structural and semantic threat scores whose interaction term was analysed rather than assumed, and it corrects the characterisation of that term from an amplifier to an agreement gate, supplying the algebraic argument that the hybrid score cannot exceed the mean of its components. Second, it contributes HTSA-E, an escalation refinement whose confidence threshold is derived from data as the lowest value at which no legitimate training item escalates, and which closes a structural-cleanliness ceiling that allowed weak safety evidence to veto strong threat evidence. Third, it demonstrates a deny-by-default architecture for content-level detection in which every verdict is auditable and every indeterminate check is treated as a failure, giving concrete form to a posture that the reviewed literature discusses mainly at the network level. Fourth, it documents five measurement artefacts and shows that three of them are instances of a single principle, the improper conversion of absent evidence into evidence of safety, which is a methodological finding about building detection systems under deny-by-default. Fifth, it corrects the rule-scoring formula to normalise over the applicable indicator set, without which a reputable bare domain is misclassified as a medium risk.

## 5.4 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the strength of the findings and should be read alongside them. The most consequential is that the evaluation was truncated for time. The full corpus was not scored, because the free-tier rate limit on the semantic model degraded a large share of items to rule-only scores that were correctly excluded, and the detection results therefore rest on 857 items rather than the full planned set. The email class in particular is thin. This bears directly on the central experimental result, because the agreement gate is inert on every URL submission, on which the semantic layer abstains, so its entire measured effect lives within 129 email items, of which only 19 are phishing. The gate's value cannot be considered settled on so small a positive class, and the perfect recall and area under the curve reported for the standalone semantic condition rest on the same small class and are correspondingly unreliable. The escalation threshold introduced in HTSA-E is likewise anchored to a single authored legitimate item and would need to be re-derived on a larger clean corpus.

Further limitations follow from the design and the data. The path blind spot identified in the study is a boundary condition of structural analysis rather than a defect of this implementation, since an attacker who controls a path on a legitimate, established domain leaves no structural evidence for any structural detector to find. The evaluation used two corpus substitutions, the Phishing.Database feed in place of PhishTank and the SpamAssassin legitimate corpus in place of Enron, each for a practical reason. The hard-case corpus contains approximately 22 per cent authored items, disclosed because they were written for the study. As stated in Chapter One, the system also depends on an external model that requires connectivity, is evaluated only on English-language content, and operates as a standalone application rather than integrating with existing mail or browser infrastructure.

## 5.5 Recommendations

Arising from these findings, several recommendations are offered. The first is that the evaluation be completed on a clean run with a thicker email corpus, using either a paid model tier or more aggressive caching to avoid the rate-limit degradation that truncated the present run, so that the value of the agreement gate can be settled on an adequate positive class rather than estimated from 19 phishing emails. The second is that HTSA-E be adopted as the delivered fusion, since it closes the structural-cleanliness ceiling at no measured cost to legitimate items, with its threshold re-derived on the larger corpus. The third concerns the path blind spot: rather than attempt a path-level detector, which cannot reliably distinguish a fabricated but plausible path from a real one offline and would over-flag legitimate payment paths, the system should degrade its verdict to insufficient evidence where an entire attribute class such as the URL path was never examined, so that clearance becomes proportional to the coverage of the analysis rather than being granted in the absence of any adverse signal. This change alters cached scores and is therefore recommended for a future revision rather than applied retrospectively. The fourth recommendation is that the reporting convention of presenting precision and recall separately, rather than F1 alone, be retained, since the corrections made during this study were repeatedly visible in one of those measures while hidden in their harmonic mean.

## 5.6 Suggestions for Further Research

Several directions extend naturally from this work. The most immediate is coverage-aware scoring, in which a verdict encodes not only how threatening the examined attributes were but how much of the submission was examined at all, which would generalise the path blind spot into a principled treatment of unexamined attribute classes. A second direction is the detection of phishing on compromised or shared legitimate infrastructure, which defeats structural analysis by construction and would require reputation or behavioural signals beyond the static structure of a single submission. A third is the replacement of the fixed fusion weights with weights learned from data, and a comparison of a learned fusion against the fixed convex combination used here. Further research might also extend the semantic layer to languages other than English, evaluate the system against adversarially crafted inputs designed to evade the specific indicators it uses, and assess the durability of the deny-by-default posture and the agreement gate on live traffic at a scale beyond the benchmark corpora used in this study.
